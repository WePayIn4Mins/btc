Science Fiction usually gets there first. Cryptocurrencies are no exception.

“We’re going to issue electronic banknotes.
No paper at all?
No paper at all.
So you can spend it on the Net.
Correct.
What if you want to buy a sack of bananas?
Find a banana merchant on the Net.
Seems like paper money’d be just as good.
Paper money is traceable and perishable and has other drawbacks. Electronic banknotes are fast and anonymous.
What’s an electronic banknote look like, Randy?
Like any other digital thing: a bunch of bits.
Doesn’t that make it kind of easy to counterfeit?
Not if you have good crypto, Randy says. Which we do. How did you get it?
By hanging out with maniacs.
What kind of maniacs?
Maniacs who think that having good crypto is of near-apocalyptic importance.”

This exchange of dialogue is a pretty accurate summary of cryptocurrencies. But it’s from a novel called Cryptonomicon, by Neal Stephenson, which was published in 1999 - nine years before the creation of Bitcoin.

The only aspect of cryptocurrency Stephenson doesn't predict is blockchain. But that's because his focus is on the science of cryptotography and its relationship with money. The storylines alternate between modern times and World War II. The 1940s sections take us into the German, Japanese, American and British fighting forces. One strand vividly recreates the efforts of the allies to break Enigma and other German and Japanese ciphers, featuring Alan Turing as a character.

It was a popular and highly influential book at the time. Peter Thiel, founder of PayPal, says that it was required reading for his team. Today it remains a highly recommended read, but with one proviso. You have to be ready for a thousand-page blockbuster to take over your life for a week or so, and make you think really hard at times. No problem for any Harry Potter reader.
